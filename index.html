A group of massive rocks, weighing hundreds of pounds, moving around on their own, leaving long trails in their wake. Sounds like something straight out of a sci-fi flick, right? But this isn't fiction, it's a real-life phenomenon known as the sailing rocks.

These mysterious rocks are found in Racetrack Playa, a dry lakebed in California's Death Valley National Park.

For decades, scientists and researchers have been scratching their heads trying to figure out how these massive boulders could move by themselves, with no apparent external force.

But it's not just scientists who are intrigued by the sailing rocks. People from all over the world flock to Racetrack Playa to witness this bizarre and beautiful natural wonder.

"When I arrived at the Racetrack Playa, I never expected to witness one of nature's greatest mysteries," said John Smith, a traveler from Australia.

"After camping overnight in the area, I woke up to find that some of the massive rocks had completely moved, leaving long trails behind them. It was both eerie and fascinating, and it made me wonder what other secrets the natural world is hiding."

But in 2014, they finally cracked the code.

After several decades of study and observation, the sailing stones of Racetrack Playa were observed in motion. Scientists were able to document the environment and the factors which create movements.

A balance of specific conditions is thought to be needed for stones to move:

WATER

The surface should be flooded. Water, which must be deep enough to form floating ice during cold winter nights but shallow enough to expose the rocks.

TEMPERATURE

As nighttime temperatures plummet, the pond freezes to form thin sheets of “windowpane” ice, which must be thin enough to move freely but thick enough to maintain strength.

WIND

Light winds drive ice sheets across the playa, pushing rocks in front of them and leaving trails in the soft mud below the surface.

SUNNY DAYS

On sunny days, the ice begins to melt and break up into large floating panels.

It turns out that a combination of ice, wind, and water is responsible for the movement of the sailing rocks.

During winter, thin layers of ice form on the surface of the lakebed. As the sun warms the ice, it breaks up into thin sheets, creating a shallow layer of water. When the water freezes again, strong gusts of wind - sometimes reaching speeds of up to 90 mph - push the rocks, which slide across the lakebed, leaving their unique tracks behind.

SOME OF THE ROCKS ARE ESTIMATED TO WEIGHT AS MUCH AS 0 KILOGRAMS

But over all, there is nothing special about the sailing stone formation. They are ordinary rocks that were accidentally placed into specific conditions.

Sailing rocks are mostly composed of dolomites and granites, which were previously a part of local surrounding cliffs and outcrops.
